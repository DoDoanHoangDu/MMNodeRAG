Prostate cancer begins when cells in the prostate gland, part of the male reproductive system, grow uncontrollably. The primary risk factors include increasing age, having a family history of the disease, and certain inherited genetic mutations (such as BRCA1 and BRCA2). While early-stage (localized) prostate cancer is often slow-growing and may not cause symptoms, advanced-stage (metastatic) cancer that has spread to other parts of the body can cause symptoms like blood in the urine, bone pain, and unexplained weight loss.

Diagnosis typically involves a prostate-specific antigen (PSA) blood test and a digital rectal exam (DRE). A definitive diagnosis requires a biopsy, where tissue samples are analyzed to determine a Gleason score, which grades the cancer's aggressiveness. These test results help classify the cancer's risk level and guide treatment decisions, which for advanced cancer may include hormone therapy, chemotherapy, or radiation to slow the disease's progression and manage symptoms.
